Part i · The Problem

Banks operate inside a search space they cannot enumerate.

Modern banking decisions are not single-variable choices — they are high-dimensional, multi-objective, often non-convex problems. The question is rarely “what is the optimum?” but “among millions of nearly-equivalent optima, which is most robust?”

2⁵⁰
Possible portfolio allocations

For 50 candidate loans with binary include/exclude, the search space contains ≈ 1.13 × 10¹⁵ configurations — beyond any brute-force enumeration.

≥ 4
Conflicting objectives

Maximize expected return, minimize variance, satisfy capital ratios, preserve liquidity buffers. No single optimum exists — only a Pareto frontier of trade-offs.

∇f ⊥ ∇f
Non-differentiable surface

Regulatory constraints introduce discontinuities and discrete jumps. Gradient-based methods stall at local optima or fail to converge entirely.

A worked example

ARM Bank receives ~250 credit applications per day. Each carries a requested amount, an internal credit score, an industry tag, and a term. The credit officer must choose how much of each to approve, while staying inside a 600 M UAH daily capital envelope and respecting Basel-III ratios.

Even reducing each loan to a discrete 4-level decision (deny / 25% / 50% / 100%) yields 4²⁵⁰ ≈ 10¹⁵⁰ configurations — a number with more digits than there are atoms in the observable universe.

The search is unenumerable. It is also dynamic — yesterday's optimum is not today's, because rates and reserves change.

Part ii · Why Classical Methods Fail

Every conventional tool collapses on at least one assumption.

The optimization toolkit taught in undergraduate courses presumes structure that simply isn't there in real banking landscapes — linearity, convexity, smoothness, low dimension, or stationarity. None hold.

Method Assumption Failure mode in banking Verdict
Linear programming Linear objective & constraints Default-probability and recovery models are inherently non-linear Underfits
Gradient descent Differentiable convex surface Stuck in local optima; no gradient at hard constraint boundaries Stalls
Branch-and-bound Bounded combinatorial space with tight relaxations Exponential blow-up past ~30 dimensions Intractable
Dynamic programming Optimal substructure, low state count Curse of dimensionality at our state vector size Intractable
Monte-Carlo search No structure to exploit No memory — wastes evaluations on already-visited regions Inefficient
Simulated annealing A good cooling schedule exists Single-individual: cannot exploit population diversity Partial
Evolutionary algorithms Fitness can be evaluated, not differentiated — Match

Visual: a typical banking fitness landscape

Each peak is a locally-good portfolio; the global optimum is rarely the most prominent. Gradient methods (red) climb the nearest peak. An evolutionary population (gold) samples broadly first, then converges.

The single line that summarizes everything

“Calculus tells you where to step.
Evolution tells you where to look.”

The fundamental shift is conceptual, not computational. Classical optimization is local refinement; evolutionary computation is structured exploration. The two are complementary — and for problems with our shape, exploration is the binding constraint.

SLIDE04/ 11
Part iii · Mathematical Foundation

Evolution, formalized.

Evolutionary computation reframes optimization as a stochastic search guided by selection pressure. A population of candidate solutions iteratively improves through three operators inspired directly by natural genetics — and supported by half a century of mathematical theory.

// Formal definition Population: Pₜ = { x₁, x₂, …, xₙ } ∈ Ωⁿ Fitness: f: Ω → ℝ // any computable scalar function Goal: x* = argmaxₓ∈Ω f(x) // One generation transition Pₜ₊₁ = Replace( Pₜ, Mutate( Crossover( Select( Pₜ, f ) ) ) ) // Selection probability — fitness-proportional (roulette) Pr(xᵢ) = f(xᵢ) / Σⱼ f(xⱼ) // Convergence (informal) ∀ ε > 0, ∃ T: Pr( |max f(Pₜ) − f(x*)| < ε ) → 1 as t → T

The three operators

1

Selection

Choose parents with probability proportional to fitness. Fitter individuals reproduce more — the source of directional pressure.

2

Crossover

Recombine genetic material from two parents. Exploits structure already discovered in the population.

3

Mutation

Apply small random perturbations. Introduces novelty — preserves exploration and escapes local optima.

The exploration / exploitation balance

All search algorithms must trade off two competing goals: exploration (sampling unknown regions) and exploitation (refining known-good regions).

What makes evolutionary computation special is that it maintains both simultaneously via a population. Selection pressure exploits; mutation explores; crossover does both, depending on the population's current diversity.

SLIDE05/ 11
Part iv · Why It Works

Holland's Schema Theorem.

John Holland (1975) proved that genetic algorithms are not random wandering. They allocate search effort according to an implicit parallelism — every individual evaluation simultaneously provides evidence about many overlapping building blocks.

// Schema Theorem (Holland, 1975) m(H, t+1) ≥ m(H, t) · f(H)/f̄ · [ 1 − pc · δ(H)/(L−1) − o(H) · pm ] // Where m(H, t) = number of population members matching schema H at gen t f(H) = average fitness of schema H f̄ = average fitness of population δ(H) = defining length (distance between fixed positions) o(H) = order (number of fixed positions) pc, pm = crossover & mutation probabilities L = chromosome length

What this says, in one sentence

Short, low-order schemata with above-average fitness receive exponentially increasing trials in subsequent generations.

Translation: good partial solutions get amplified faster than bad ones decay. The algorithm doesn't need to find the global optimum directly — it just needs to discover its building blocks and let recombination assemble them.

Implicit parallelism

A population of n individuals of length L implicitly samples O(n³) schemata at once — a property no single-point method can match.

With our typical setup of n = 200, L = 32: roughly 8 × 10⁶ schemata are evaluated per generation. That is the ratio behind the speed-up over Monte-Carlo sampling.

Monte-Carlo / generation200 evals
GA effective / generation~8 000 000 schemata
Effective speed-up×40 000
“The schema theorem is the closest thing computer science has to a thermodynamic law of search — heat dissipates upward, fitness propagates downward.” — D. Goldberg, paraphrased
SLIDE06/ 11
Part v · Algorithm Walk-through

Anatomy of a single generation.

Below is the precise sequence executed by ARM Bank's production engine, annotated with the hyperparameters chosen after grid-search across 12 month-equivalent back-tests.

1

Initialize population

Generate n = 200 random candidate solutions across the feasible region. Diversity at t = 0 determines reachable optima.

2

Evaluate fitness

Compute f(xᵢ) for each individual. In banking: simulate the portfolio's expected return under a 1 000-scenario stochastic risk model.

3

Selection · tournament k = 3

Draw three random individuals; the fittest becomes a parent. Repeat until n parents are chosen. Preserves diversity better than roulette.

4

Crossover · uniform, pc = 0.85

For each pair of parents, swap each gene independently with 50% probability. Produces offspring inheriting traits from both.

5

Mutation · Gaussian, σ = 0.05

With probability pm = 0.18, perturb each gene by 𝒩(0, σ²). Maintains exploration; prevents premature convergence.

6

Replacement · elitism e = 2

Carry the top-2 individuals into the next generation untouched. Guarantees monotonic improvement of the best-so-far.

7

Repeat until convergence

Stop when best fitness stagnates for T = 50 generations or a 5-second wall-clock budget expires. Return the elite individual as x*.

Part vi · Application at ARM Bank

Credit-portfolio optimization, in production.

ARM Bank issues consumer and SME credit lines daily. Each new application competes for a limited capital budget under regulatory risk constraints. We frame this as a multi-objective optimization problem solved by an evolutionary engine, called from the credit-officer dashboard.

Decision variables

For each candidate loan i in the daily queue:

xᵢ ∈ [0, 1] // fraction of requested amount approved

A 32-loan day yields a continuous ℝ32 search space — at 8-bit precision per gene, that is ≈ 10⁹⁶ effective configurations.

Multi-objective fitness

f(x) = w₁·E[R(x)] − w₂·σ(x) − w₃·VaR₉₅(x) − λ·Φ(x)

E[R] expected return · σ portfolio volatility · VaR95 95-percentile loss · Φ aggregated constraint penalty. Weights wi are calibrated quarterly against ALCO targets.

Constraints encoded as soft penalties

Basel III

Capital adequacy

Risk-weighted assets ≤ 12 × Tier-1 capital. Penalty: λ1(RWA − 12·T1)+²

Liquidity

LCR floor

≥ 18% of deposits as on-demand reserves. Penalty: λ2(0.18·D − Reserve)+²

Concentration

Sector exposure

No single industry > 25% of total exposure. Penalty: λ3 · max(0, maxs es − 0.25)²

KYC / AML

Hard exclusions

Sanction-list applicants are removed before entering the search space — never softened.

Term mix

Maturity ladder

Maintain target weighted-average maturity within ±10% of policy. Penalty: λ4(WAM − target)²

Fairness

Disparate impact

Approval-rate ratio across protected groups within EEOC's 4/5 rule. Penalty: λ5·max(0, 0.8 − ratio)²

Part vii · System Architecture

How the engine plugs into the bank.

The evolutionary optimizer runs as an isolated micro-service. It consumes the daily application queue, the current capital state and a market snapshot — and emits an approval vector, a Pareto frontier of alternatives, and a complete audit trail ready for the credit officer to ratify.

Monitoring

Part x · Conclusion & Roadmap

A general-purpose lever for hard banking decisions.

Evolutionary computation is not a single algorithm — it is a paradigm. Wherever the bank faces a decision space too large to enumerate and too irregular for calculus, the same engine, re-skinned with a new fitness function, applies.

Immediate next applications

  • ATM cash-replenishment scheduling — minimize idle cash
  • Branch staffing & opening-hour assignment
  • Marketing-campaign segment selection
  • Fraud-detection feature engineering (genetic programming)
  • Treasury hedging-instrument mix
  • Loan-collection contact-strategy sequencing

Why now

Modern CPUs evaluate 200-individual populations in milliseconds. The cost of one generation is a rounding error against the cost of one mis-priced loan.

The mathematics has been settled for fifty years. The bottleneck was always the will to deploy — and the scaffolding to interpret the output. Both are now in place at ARM Bank.
